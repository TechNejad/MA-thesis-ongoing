Abstract

Large Language Models are being deployed as agents in multi-agent simulations, where their behaviour depends largely on the roles scripted into their system prompts. These roles are not merely functional specifications; they encode normative behavioural expectations about what agents should do, how they should treat others, and what the context requires of them. Yet no framework exists for analysing normative roles and how they unfold across domains. This thesis develops a conceptual ontology for examining how such roles are designed, what normative content they carry, and what outcomes they produce. A systematic literature review of 724 papers yielded 38 studies, coded under a four-layer schema into 93 role conditions, 210 entities, and 503 relational triples. The results reveal partial normative content in most role designs, meaningful differences across simulation domains, and failure modes in nearly half of all role conditions. The thesis contributes (i) a reframing of system prompts as normative documents, (ii) a conceptual ontology of normative role design, and (iii) a reusable coding schema for analysing roles across multi-agent LLM studies. Together, they offer a basis for designing, evaluating, and governing normative role design for LLM agents.

Methodology

The research proceeds in two phases: a systematic literature review that assembles the corpus, and an ontology construction process that codes and formalises it.

  • Systematic Literature Review. 1,056 records from Scopus and Web of Science, reduced to 724 unique entries after deduplication. Three screening stages (title and abstract, full-text, quality assessment) narrowed the corpus to 40 studies, of which 38 were ultimately coded.
  • Four-Layer Coding. Each role condition is coded across four analytical layers: (A) normative role design, (B) simulation setup, (C) outcome analysis, and (D) failure modes. Layer A additionally scores five binary normative indicators, yielding a 0–5 normative-content score.
  • Ontology Construction. The 93 role conditions across the 38 studies yielded 210 entities, 15 relation types, and 503 subject–relation–object triples. The ontology is organised around seven competency questions that trace the causal chain from prompt design to agent behaviour to collective outcomes and failures.
